Predicting Tip-Giving Behavior in NYC Yellow Taxis

Using Distributed Machine Learning with Apache Spark — PCA, Random Forest, and 140 Million Trip Records

Nadine Marcus · John Naughton · Junda Su · Angela Watson
Section 01

Introduction

The NYC Taxi and Limousine Commission (TLC) publishes one of the largest publicly available urban mobility datasets in the world — over 1 billion trip records spanning 2009 to 2023. This project uses the 2019–2021 subset (140 million trips) to predict whether a taxi rider will leave a tip. While this may initially seem narrow, it opens onto broad questions in labor economics, behavioral science, and platform design.

Why This Project?

Tipping is a widely-studied but poorly-understood economic behavior. In the United States, norms vary enormously by context, culture, and payment mechanism. For taxi drivers — who depend on tips as a substantial fraction of their income — a predictive model of tipping behavior has genuine economic value. NYC taxi data captures a dense, high-resolution picture of urban human mobility: millions of individual journeys encoding time, distance, price, and payment choice.

Broader Impact of a Good Predictive Model

  • Drivers and cooperatives: Dispatch systems could route drivers toward high-tip-probability zones or times, improving earnings without increasing total trips.
  • Regulators and labor advocates: Understanding which trip types reliably attract tips illuminates structural inequities in gig-worker compensation.
  • Platform designers: In-app tipping prompts can be personalized based on trip context to increase tip rates.
  • Urban planners: Aggregate tipping rates across neighborhoods and times proxy rider willingness-to-pay, relevant to equity analysis.
  • Academic researchers: A reproducible, scalable pipeline for modeling tip behavior on 100M+ records advances the methodology for studying large-scale behavioral datasets.

Why Did This Require Big Data and Distributed Computing?

At 140 million records, this dataset is well beyond the practical capacity of single-machine workflows:

  • Memory constraints: Loading 140M rows of floating-point features into a single NumPy array would require ~15–20 GB of RAM, making in-memory shuffling for train/test splits infeasible on most workstations.
  • Training time: Scikit-learn's Random Forest is single-threaded. A 50-tree, depth-10 forest on 110M training rows would take many hours on a single core. PySpark distributes tree construction across executor cores, reducing this to minutes.
  • Preprocessing at scale: Timestamp parsing, binning, imputation, and encoding applied to 140M rows. Spark's lazy evaluation and DAG optimization fuse these into a single data pass.
  • Distributed PCA: pyspark.ml.feature.PCA estimates the covariance matrix across all executors in parallel. Running PCA on a 110M × 18 matrix is conceptually straightforward but requires a full distributed pass over the data.

Without Spark/Ray: This analysis would require either downsampling to ~1–2M records and accepting non-representative results, or prohibitively expensive cloud compute. Spark on SDSC Expanse (24 cores, 64 GB RAM) enabled full-dataset processing in a tractable time window.

2.4 Model 2: PCA + Random Forest

The second model adds a dimensionality reduction stage before classification. Features were first standardised then projected onto the top k=10 principal components using pyspark.ml.feature.PCA. The same Random Forest architecture was applied to the compressed representation.

Section 04

Discussion

4.1 How Believable Are These Results?

A 97% accuracy on 140 million records sounds extraordinary. It warrants careful scrutiny.

The 97.17% accuracy and 0.9662 AUC are impressive — but a critical examination reveals the likely source of this performance. Payment type is a near-perfect proxy for the label. The feature payment_type encodes whether a rider paid by credit card (1) or cash (2). Cash-paying riders physically cannot leave an electronic tip, so their tip_amount is always 0.0 in the dataset. The model has effectively learned to detect this structural data-entry pattern rather than generalized human tipping behavior. This is a form of data leakage — the label is partially encoded in a feature.

A rigorous follow-up would re-run both models excluding payment_type. We expect AUC would drop meaningfully, giving a truer measure of what the model has learned about human behavior.

Additional factors worth noting:

  • Class imbalance: A naive classifier predicting "always tip" would achieve 70.5% accuracy. Our model is far better than this baseline, suggesting it is learning genuine structure beyond payment type.
  • Temporal test: The temporal holdout (train <2021, test on 2021) yielded AUC of 0.9569 vs. 0.9667 for random split — a small drop suggesting mild concept drift that the random split artificially hides.
  • 10% sample: All training used ~13.8M rows (10% of the dataset). Results may differ at full scale, particularly for rare patterns.

4.2 Fitting Analysis

Both models sit firmly in the well-generalized region of the bias-variance tradeoff. The train-test AUC gap is 0.0006 for Model 1 and effectively zero for Model 2 — no meaningful overfitting. With AUC consistently above 0.95 and accuracy above 97%, neither model is underfitting either.

The PCA model trades a tiny amount of information (losing 40% of variance) for a slightly more constrained representation. The Δ AUC of 0.003 confirms this shift is negligible in practice. This is consistent with theory: Random Forest already handles correlated features via random subspace sampling at each split, so PCA provides less benefit than it would for a linear model.

4.3 What Does the PCA Analysis Reveal?

  • PC1 (12.3%) is dominated by fare-related features (fare_amount, total_amount, trip_duration) — the main axis is expensive vs. cheap trips.
  • PCs 2–10 each contribute 4.5–8.8% — a relatively flat scree plot indicating distributed, moderate structure rather than a few dominant axes.
  • 60% cumulative variance at k=10 is low given only ~18 original features — suggesting the features are not highly collinear. Random Forest is well-equipped for this low-redundancy structure.
  • 90% variance requires 20+ components — near the full feature set, meaning PCA achieves little compression at the 90% level.

4.4 Shortcomings and Critiques

Data leakage: payment_type is likely the primary driver of model performance. Excluding it is necessary before claiming the model has learned generalizable tipping behavior.

10% sampling: Training on ~13.8M rows (10% of 138M) was necessary for computational efficiency. The Spark pipeline is designed to scale, and full-scale training should be the next step.

PCA k selection: Only 60% of variance is captured at k=10. A more principled approach would select k based on a cumulative variance threshold (e.g., 90%). With k≈20 capturing 90% variance, the PCA model might close the 0.003 AUC gap entirely.

No feature importance back-projection: The current analysis does not back-project RF feature importances from PCA space to the original feature space. This step is critical for any deployment-facing application where interpretability matters.

Temporal coverage: The dataset spans 2019–2021, including the COVID-19 pandemic. A model trained on pre-COVID data may generalize poorly to pandemic-era or post-pandemic conditions, when ridership patterns changed dramatically.

Section 05

Conclusion

Model Summary

Model 1 (Random Forest, full features) achieves AUC-ROC of 0.9662, 97.17% accuracy, 96.32% precision, and 99.79% recall. The confusion matrix shows 1,944,096 true positives and only 4,069 false negatives — a miss rate of 0.21%. The negligible train-test gap confirms excellent generalization.

Model 2 (PCA + Random Forest) achieves AUC-ROC of 0.9636 — only 0.003 lower — while compressing ~18 features to 10 principal components capturing 60.1% of variance. Tipping can be predicted with a substantially more compact representation at minimal performance cost.

Future Directions

  1. Remove payment_type and re-evaluate. Most important immediate improvement to test for data leakage.
  2. Full-dataset training (138M rows). The pipeline is already designed for scale.
  3. Gradient Boosted Trees (GBT). Typically outperforms RF on structured tabular data.
  4. Logistic Regression on PCA features. Linear models benefit more from orthogonalized inputs.
  5. K-Means on PCA components. Discover trip archetypes — urban hops, airport runs, night-time rides — for cluster-specific models.
  6. SVD via RowMatrix.computeSVD. Compare against PySpark PCA at full 138M-row scale.
  7. Hyperparameter search with CrossValidator. Grid over numTrees, maxDepth, and PCA k.

Reflections on Big Data Processing

What we learned: Processing 140 million records is qualitatively different from 140,000. The primary challenge is not raw computation but pipeline design — lazy evaluation, partitioning strategy, and avoiding unnecessary data materializations. Spark's DAG optimization handles many of these automatically, but understanding why .count() triggers full recomputation required a meaningful shift in thinking from the pandas workflow.

How distributed computing changed our approach: The availability of 24 cores on Expanse changed what was tractable. Rather than choosing between "analyze a small sample well" or "analyze the full data poorly," we could train a 50-tree Random Forest on 110 million rows. This changes the scientific question from "what can we do with a sample?" to "what does the full population tell us?" — a fundamentally more powerful position.

What we would explore with more time: With a larger Expanse allocation, we would run full-dataset (138M row) training, implement distributed hyperparameter search with Ray Tune, and extend the analysis to the full TLC dataset (2009–2023, ~1.5 billion rows) to study how tipping behavior has evolved — across the smartphone era, the rise of rideshare competition, and COVID-19. The distributed infrastructure makes this scientifically tractable; what it requires is time and allocation budget.
